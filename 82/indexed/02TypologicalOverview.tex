\section{Morphology}
\label{sec:2-2}

Palula morphology is suffixing, and formatives are almost exclusively concatenative \citep{bickel_fusion_2013}, with a~moderately high degreee of synthesis \citep{bickel_inflectional_2013}. 


Nouns are inflected for number (singular, plural) and case (\isi{nominative}, \isi{oblique}, \isi{genitive}). In most of the declensional classes, \isi{nominative} plural and \isi{oblique} singular are cumulated into a~single formative \citep{bickel_exponence_2013}. The \isi{genitive} (at least in the plural) can be analysed as suffixed to the \isi{oblique} rather than to the \isi{nominative} stem. The \isi{noun} exemplified in \tabref{tab:2-nouns} is \textit{ṣinɡ} `horn'.

There are three main functions of the \isi{oblique} case of nouns: a) as the \isi{transitive} \isi{subject} in the \isi{perfective} (i.e., as an \isi{ergative} case marker); b) as the form to which postpositions are added; and c) as a~\isi{locative}. A number of other case"=like functions (such as recipients) and more peripheral arguments appear as postpositional phrases.


Palula displays core"=case asymmetry \citep{iggesen_asymmetrical_2013}, within the category of nouns as well as for NPs in general (more on pronouns below). While most nouns (those belonging to the two major \textit{a}- and \textit{i}-declensions) make a~\isi{nominative}"=\isi{oblique} distinction, one declensional class (the \textit{m}-\isi{declension}) in particular does not make this distinction at all, whereas some of the pronouns make an~even more fine"=tuned \isi{nominative}"=\isi{accusative}"=\isi{oblique} distinction, as seen in \tabref{tab:2-case}.

Palula has a~fairly typical Indo"=European two"=\isi{gender} system, which is primarily \isi{sex}"=based \citep{corbett_sex-based_2013}. A \isi{noun} is either masculine or feminine, a~property established through morphological agreement. The basis for \isi{gender assignment} is semantic as well as formal \citep{corbett_systems_2013}.

\label{sec:2-3}

Word order in Palula is typically head"=final (\tabref{tab:2-worder}). This is seen in the word order in \isi{noun} phrases (determiner--\isi{noun}, adjective--\isi{noun}, numeral--\isi{noun}, gen\-i\-tive--\isi{noun}), \isi{adjective} phrases (adjunct--\isi{adjective}) and in adpositional phrases (\isi{noun} phrase--\isi{postposition}). As far as entire clauses are concerned, the word order (or rather constituent order) is more flexible, but the most frequent and pragmatically unmarked order is \isi{intransitive} subject--verb, \isi{transitive} subject--verb, and direct object--verb.

As far as \isi{alignment} is concerned, Palula displays an~intricate split system. In the \isi{perfective} categories (\isi{Simple Past}, \isi{Perfect} and \isi{Pluperfect}), the pattern is essentially \isi{ergative}, as seen in example (\ref{ex:2-1}), with a~non"=nominatively marked agent"=\isi{subject} and \isi{verbal agreement} with the feminine \isi{direct object}. In the non"=\isi{perfective} categories (\isi{Future}, \isi{Present} and Past \isi{Imperfective}), in contrast, it is essentially \isi{accusative}, which can be observed from the nominatively marked agent"=\isi{subject} in (\ref{ex:2-2}), which is also the NP that the \isi{transitive} verb agrees with in \isi{gender} and number.

\end{exe}

Sentences lacking an~overt \isi{copula} are allowed, and for \isi{predicate} nominals in the \isi{Present} \isi{tense}, as the one shown in (\ref{ex:2-5}), they are typical.

\begin{exe}
\ex
\label{ex:2-5}
\gll míi báabu áak zamindaár míiš \\ 
\textsc{1sg.gen} father \textsc{idef} farmer man \\
\glt `My father is a~farmer.' (A:OUR002)
\end{exe}
Although it is possible to conjoin clauses with a~conjunctive suffix (also used for conjoining \isi{noun} phrases), other strategies are preferred, such as \isi{juxtaposition} for symmetrical clauses or the overwhelmingly favoured \isi{Converb} construction, exemplified in (\ref{ex:2-6}), which is used for a~great variety of same"=\isi{subject} \isi{clause} combinations.

\begin{exe}
\ex
\label{ex:2-6}
\gll tíi ba {\ob}bhun wha-í ba{\cb} {\ob}so mhaás muṭ-í bhun wheel-í ba{\cb} {\ob}teeṇíi ɡhooṣṭ-á the ɡhin-í{\cb} ɡáu \\
\textsc{3sg.obl} \textsc{top} down come.down-\textsc{cv} \textsc{top} \textsc{def.msg.nom} meat tree-\textsc{gen} down take.down-\textsc{cv} \textsc{top} \textsc{refl} house-\textsc{obl} to take-\textsc{cv} go.\textsc{pfv.msg} \\
\glt `He came down [having come down], took down the meat from the tree [having taken down the meat from the tree], and brought it to his house.' (B:SHB762)
\end{exe}

In complex constructions, the unmarked order is a~\isi{complement clause} followed by (or embedded in) the main \isi{clause}, as in (\ref{ex:2-7}), and similarly an~adverbial \isi{clause} followed by (or, again, embedded in) the main \isi{clause}, as in (\ref{ex:2-8}). 

\begin{exe}
\ex
\label{ex:2-7}
\gll \textbf{neečíir} \textbf{theníi-e} díiš-e xalk-íim xwaaíš thíil-i \\
	hunt do.\textsc{vn"=gen} village\textsc{-gen} people\textsc{-pl.obl} desire do.\textsc{pfv-f} \\
\glt `People in the village wanted to go hunting.' (B:AVA200)
\end{exe}

\begin{exe}
\ex
\label{ex:2-8}
\gll \textbf{raaǰaá} \textbf{múṛ-u} \textbf{ta} putr-óom tasíi hukumát bulooṣṭéel-i \\
	king die.\textsc{pfv"=msg} \textsc{sub} son\textsc{-pl.obl} \textsc{3sg.gen} government snatch.\textsc{pfv-f} \\
\glt `When the king died, the sons seized the power.' (A:MAB003)
\end{exe}

However, a~post"=posed construction with the \isi{complementiser} \textit{ki} is also commonly used (\ref{ex:2-9}), especially for utterance complements.

\begin{exe}
\ex
\label{ex:2-9}
\gll ɡhueeṇíi-am maníit-u ki \textbf{ni} \textbf{bíiḍ-a} \textbf{zinaawúr} \textbf{xálaka} \textbf{hín-a}\\
	Pashtun-\textsc{pl.obl} say.\textsc{pfv-msg} \textsc{comp} \textsc{3pl.prox.nom} much-\textsc{mpl} wild people be.\textsc{prs"=mpl} \\
\glt `The Pashtuns said, ``These are very wild people.''' (A:CHA008)
\end{exe}

Polar interrogatives are formed with a~clitical sentence"=final question particle \textit{ee} (B \textit{aa}), as in (\ref{ex:2-10}), whereas an~\isi{indefinite}"=\isi{interrogative} \isi{pronoun} (or other proform), such as \textit{kasée} (B) `whose' in (\ref{ex:2-11}), is used in content interrogatives. 

\begin{exe}
\ex
\label{ex:2-10}
\gll ux-á díi khooǰóol-u ki tu insaán\textbf{=ee}\\
	camel-\textsc{obl} from ask.\textsc{pfv"=msg} \textsc{comp} \textsc{2sg.nom} human.being=\textsc{q} \\
\glt `He asked the camel, ``Are you a~man?''' (A:KIN007)
\end{exe}


\begin{exe}
\ex
\label{ex:2-11}
\gll aní \textbf{kasée} ziaarat-í thaní \\
	\textsc{prox.3pl.nom} whose shrine-\textsc{pl} \textsc{quot} \\
\glt `Whose shrines are these?' (B:FOR026)
\end{exe}

Negation is formed with a~separate and invariable negative particle \textit{na}, preceding the \isi{predicate} (\ref{ex:2-12}).

\begin{exe}
\ex
\label{ex:2-12}
\gll muṣṭúk-a xálak-a dhii-á díi \textbf{na} khooǰ-óon de \\
	of.past-\textsc{mpl} people-\textsc{pl} daughter-\textsc{obl} from \textsc{neg} ask-\textsc{3pl} \textsc{pst} \\
\glt `People in the old days were not asking their daughter [who she wanted to marry].' (A:MAR018)
\end{exe}
