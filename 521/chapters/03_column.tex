    \ParallelLText{\noindent \textit{On Yom oye kaduk odo. Kaduk, ye go ye amban yanop. Yu, amban, yu taman ko mim an.}}
    \ParallelRText{\noindent This is the story about the origin of the bat. There once was a man who had a wife. She, the wife, she had a brother.}
\end{Parallel}

    \vspace{.4cm}

\begin{Parallel}{0.47\textwidth}{0.47\textwidth}
    \ParallelLText{\noindent \textit{Mim jadi, kedone om wine kolonggoden. Ke, ``ade yanop kole, kup taman yanop kole, om ko alume!'' Ma ye go Awin ambip wenen. Baat, jadi ye baat ko Awin ambip wonoden. Wonoden got yi bet mo tiip. Tionip ket, wonoden gole tiip, koe. Tionip a, ko, om ko, be mene, om alume wane, yale ko, mananip e, ``Ah, mok kolip a, ya? Kip ko kolip a. Ye aip wonoden oyip a. Monok ga ya'', enggimo, yi go alabeyimo, om ko, om ko yi aip kaye kemok balin.}}
    \ParallelRText{\noindent One day, the man cut down a sago tree and said to his wife: ``In this place, you have relatives and you have a brother. You can make sago without me.'' Then he went off to the Awin region. He went off and she had to stay alone with her brother. There was the sago which they processed and brought home. After they made the sago, they stored it and she would announce: ``Oh, you just wait, yeah? You all just wait for him to come back. Let him come back first before we eat it''. And the adults would withhold the sago, they never gave the sago to the children.}
\end{Parallel}

    \vspace{.4cm}

\begin{Parallel}{0.47\textwidth}{0.47\textwidth}
    \ParallelLText{\noindent \textit{Alidan yi bet, yika yi aip kaye kemok balin. Odep mo om ko, ``Moyon mok om nup aip kayip ko anem, ah!'' Om ko wene alumanip. Wene be mananip. Ombenep ko tane wane, wene kayanip? Ih, yi aip kaye kemok balin! Om ko yika be kane madet mo. Okemodonipket, eyuk.}}
    \ParallelRText{\noindent The parents themselves never gave it to them. But the children begged: ``Oh, come on, please give us some sago to eat!'' The woman and her brother would go and chop sago. They would bring it back home. They would take out the ``Ombenep'' and would they share any sago? -- No, they never gave it to the children! They collected the sago to save it for later. And they kept doing it.}
\end{Parallel}

    \vspace{.4cm}

\begin{Parallel}{0.47\textwidth}{0.47\textwidth}
    \ParallelLText{\noindent \textit{Kedo om bat wenebip, koe. Om bat winip kot kedonipket a: ``Enggon, midep okunanup, midep okunanup?'' Kedo i kumunggon gi. I ninggi yi go alopmim, i on Yom kelon. On Kudumbop kelon, on Dimin kelon, on Tinggulut kelon, iya. Yi go kanin bet an, kanin bet kole. Eyuk.}}
    \ParallelRText{\noindent One day when the adults were off to make sago, the children had a discussion. ``Guys, what shall we do, what shall we do?'', the older brother said. There were three brothers, the older brother became a big bat in the end.{\footnotemark} One of them became a ``Kudumbop'' bat, another became a ``Dimin'' bat and the last one became a ``Tinggulut'' bat. They actually were four children.}\footnotetext{There were actually four brothers. The speaker corrects this mistake at the end of the paragraph.}
\end{Parallel}

    \vspace{.4cm}

\begin{Parallel}{0.47\textwidth}{0.47\textwidth}
    \ParallelLText{\noindent \textit{Kedonip a, Aluptem tunuk bip koe. Aluptem tunuk ko beonip, nup otbop odo Aluptem an. Kedo Aluptem tunuk bip koe, Aluptem tunuk bip. Kedo yemen tim watmip. Kedo yemen ot watmip. Yemen embit ogo, wame wale, ko, ``Enggon kedep adep onongmanup ko?'' -- ``Ah enggon, okune onongmin.'' Bulut bet kanenip a, onongmonip, kane tandingge wane, yi kiling ambokono teme alebele, yi yon bedet, yon a tinggi kelegane bele, ``Kede adep onongmanup?'' -- ``Ah, enggon!'' I bet kedo kulubeyon. ``Ekine adep onongmip.'' Aluptem tunuk ko tandingge wane, yemen dim bet. Yemen tim bet kane, bulut ko telene, ekunen. Yemen dim bet, kane kombe, bulut aip Aluptem tunuk ombet telenimo, telenimo, telenimo, telenimo, okemonip wene wene ko, bedet odo, enkaen, nong Tengge enkaen oyen. Nong tengge adi go ekunen. Okmot adep ekunoden ombet bip. Ombet beonipket kane yemen tim oya talaip. Yemen tim bet talaip ko anggo aip an, atma anggo aip an, atma anggo aip an. Ko, tip an.}}
    \ParallelRText{\noindent They took some sticks of a Genemon tree. Then they took some bars of a taro tree. Finally, they picked some taro leaves. After picking the taro leaves, one of them asked: ``Guys, how is this done correctly?'' -- ``Oh guys, we make it like this!'' So they took some sticks, connected them and then they stood and looked at their faces and their toes, they looked at their feet and hands. ``How can we make it?'' â€“ ``Oh, guys, look here'', so the older brother taught them how it was done. They connected Genemon sticks with the taro bar. They took a taro bar and inserted the sticks. They just took the taro bar and there were some sticks, they would insert those Genemon sticks, stuck it in and just repeated this until they had the claws, like a hook. There is a kind of rattan called ``Nong tengge''. The top of Nong tengge is pointy like a hook. They took some of them and they stuck them on the taro bars. There were five on one side and five on the other side, so it was enough.}
\end{Parallel}

    \ParallelLText{\noindent \textit{Ege yemen dim an. Tapi yemen dim tem wom odo Aluptem tunuk ombet temetkaip. Temetkaip, onongmonip tip mo. Belenggono ya wene, manakono ya wene, manakono iyen, mimkono eyangga eyen. Mak kono eya odo, mak kono odo oyen. Eya onongmonip a, temetka wane, ko, bulu a, kimi a, onongmonip kedo kodolok a wene, kedo kenedem a, kidiptem a, monggodem a, onongmonip, ``Enggon ninggiduk ode?'' -- ``Aih, enggon ninggiduk odo.'' Oya go bip ki. Ombet bogilipket, bionipket, be kane talaip. Talae wane ko, wolobogolip kedo wane, amunggun mim ombet okune onomene bilipten. Onongmogolip kedo alendelogonipket, ``Mok aih!'' Kedo: ``Aih, enggon!'' -- ``Ih enggon, tip an!'', enggon. Alendelonip kedo wane kedo wonon, iya. On Yom kedo wane kedo wonon. ``Ne tamana, tip an'', enggon.}}
    \ParallelRText{\noindent It was a taro bar. But they stuck in it with the Genemon sticks. They stuck and they formed all body parts from that: The shoulder bones, then the ribs, these ribs and the spine. But at this bone here, that was the thighbone.{\footnotemark} They set it here and after they set it, they made wings and fur, and then went on to the head and everything around. Then the ears, nose, mouth -- they made them all. ``Guys, what about the teeth?'' -- ``Oh guys, the teeths should be ...'' They made them and put them on. After they had put them on, they broke it and then they kept making all the body parts the whole day. They made them all and finally, they were standing there. ``Oh wow! Wow, guys!'', one of them said. ``Oh guys, it is working!'' They others were still standing, but he was flying already. The bat was flying. ``Oh! My brothers, it is working'', he said.}\footnotetext{In this part, the speaker points at his own body to illustrate.}
\end{Parallel}

\begin{Parallel}{0.47\textwidth}{0.47\textwidth}
    \ParallelLText{\noindent \textit{Kedo ma aip. ``Ma kep ko kedo ma wane, kedo wene!'' Ye mok. Eh, yanam an, kedo wane kedo wonon kole. Ah, mok tana, kep ko! Kep aip ma wane kedo wene! ``Eh, tip an!'' Ta kedo: ``Aih, tip an!'', ko. Wane kedo wenenip a, tip mo kol-on jadi,ko.}}
    \ParallelRText{\noindent Then it was someone else's turn. ``You there, just try to fly!'' -- ``Hey, yeah! It is working'' and he was flying. ``Oh, come on boy, it's your turn! It's your turn to fly out now!'' -- ``Oh, it's working!'' And again, ``It's working'', that's it. They flew out and everything was completed very well, that's it.}
\end{Parallel}

    \ParallelLText{\noindent \textit{Alidan odo, baat aip Awin wonoden aip odo, ye ka otbop odo yeman. Odo wot keloden yeman an. On Yom kilipten odo, ambi amban yi tana go kanin aip an. Okunenipket om, om ye madan bet a. Okunenipket tiip. Tiip ko wene, opne wenembelen, ma yi go kedonip. Amtalang eya, awit a, amko ya, amteng a, amtalang a, yimindamen winipten. Yimindalai wene kolip e yem tiip ko. Tionip kluk, otbop aip balin. Ta om odo, ``Psh!'' yonbin bet kombipten go okune: ``Pfbrrr!'' wani odo okune, amkutmon adep okune. Wadi mene amidak bon wene wadi bet tole wano bet tole wom odo om kombipten go okune: ``Pssss!'' Om on. Moyon mok!}}
    \ParallelRText{\noindent But now let's come back to the parents. I told you about them just recently. It was the story where the brother-in-law went to the Awin region. That was about the origin of the Moon.{\footnotemark} In the story about the origin of the bat, the married couple had four children. The children did what I have been telling you because of the sago. They stayed at home quietly until it was getting dark but then they started. They began to hang themselves on the roof, the rafter and the shelf. They hang themselves up and stayed quiet. They stayed quiet, they did not talk. But the sago, ``Psh!'', when they kicked it, it went like this ``Pfbrrr!'' It went down like this, it bursted to dust like this. It went up and down to the floor, up and down, and they dropped the sago, and it went like this: ``Pssss!'', the sago. ``Oh my godness!''}\footnotetext{The story and characters seems to be overlapping with the story \textit{The origin of the moon} (\chapref{text:moon})}

    \ParallelLText{\noindent \textit{Menenip ko, ambonkim wane kalegolip ko, ``Tana wenebip kot nea ka?'' -- ``Aih! Ting-ting an! Edo kedep adep an gi? Tana, eyom ede? Aih, kadek-kadek an.'' -- ``Wenebip kot, aih mok wenebok balin an!'' Wane kalenip, Pung! yon bet tanale kombe. ``Moyon, tanawat kip ko om ege kede adep kemip ke?'' -- ``Enggon o, om odo yo, baip ko angga idik mo keloden.'' -- ``Ih! Ena moyon, tana-tana yah!'' Enggipten got, ``Prrrrr'', kedo wenebip. Kedo ambonkim ombet. Mim mo mim mo kolem kedo wenebonip, kedo wenebonip, wene wene wene. On kudumbop enggaip ko kedo wane wonon e, ``Aiih! Tenop ege anggen eyen, tenop bet kanem o.'' Kolem anggudom kono ege buluna enggip kot ye go kobo ut keloden. Kole on kudumbop ko ye go kobo ut an. Kobo ut keloden. Okuneget wenebip.}}
    \ParallelRText{\noindent When the parents came back home, they did not notice any sign of their children. ``Did the children leave?'' -- ``Well, the door is closed firmly! What does this mean? Children, are you in here? Oh, it is quiet in there.'' -- ``Did they go out? Oh no, they did not go out!'' They opened the door and ``Pung!'', they kicked it open. ``Oh no, stupid children, what did you do to the sago?'' -- ``Oh friend, the sago, they tore it into pieces!'' -- ``Oh! Mother! Those damn children!'' That's how their scolding went on and then ``Prrrrr!'', the bats went out through the door. They went out one by one. The Kudumbop bat flew around and the parents said: ``Oh! Let's take the fire tongs that are lying here.'' They tried to hit it exactly here at its spine and so it got a small waist. So, from that day on the Kudumbop bat has a lean waist. And they went out just like that.}

Ye go wananon go ``wauk-wauk wauk-wauk''. ``Pak-pak pak-pak'', ah, uyen. On Kudumbop odo ye odo oyen. Odo on Kudumbop an. On Dimin odo mene opnon bet a: ``peet-peet peet-peet'' ombet. Odo on Dimin an, nup otbop bet an. Ma Tinggulut odo opnon e, bong-bong ege, eya wane towenebene bele kemip. On Tinggulut an. Okunen kole. On Yom odo om yeman bet kemonip ket, on Yom kelebip, tana-tana odo.}}
    \ParallelRText{\noindent The one who flew away first was the Yom bat. But the one she hit with a big fire tongs was the Kudumbop bat. At night, when it flies, it makes a sound like: ``Wauk-wauk wauk-wauk''. When it flies ``Wuk-wuk'' and ``Pak-pak'', that is it. That is the Kudumbop bat. But the Dimin bat usually comes in the evening and makes a sound like: ``Peet-peet peet-peet.'' It is called the Dimin bat in our language. Finally, the Tinggulut bat, when it becomes evening these small bats usually keep flying around here. It's the Tinggulut bat. And that's all of them. They became bats because of the sago. They were children and turned into bats.}
\end{Parallel}

    \ParallelRText{\noindent Until this day, we do not eat sago when hunting bats. Our ancestors never ate sago. Do not eat sago! Do not drink water! When planning to hunt bats, they usually go without all this. When they go to hunt, they would leave the women and children at home. They would throw a magic spell. And when they find some bats on a Mudut tree, they would cast a certain magic spell. The magic spell goes like this: They first go and urinate. It's for staying calm while shooting. (Nowadays, the foreigners have a gun. A foreign gun, an air soft gun.) They would urinate and whisper the magic spell: ``Lady, lady Tom, lady Tomkon Kayon, you sit here and you sit and stretch your legs like a cross and give milk to your child because I am the one who comes to you.'' He speaks his name and then he says: ``Let me polish it'' and he urinates and then he rubs it at the tree and then he would shoot his arrows.}
